    • folklore, Taylor's first surprise album, was released at the height of the COVID-19 pandemic during the time when she would have been performing her sixth concert tour, Loverfest.

    For the first time in her career, Taylor was inspired by fiction. During quarantine, she gave herself permission to "make music without any rules", and so folklore was born, and then 5 months later, her sister album, evermore.

    folklore was recorded entirely from Taylor's home, in a makeshift studio she named Kitty Committee. Her collaborators, Jack Antonoff, Aaron Dessner (of The National), and Justin Vernon (of Bon Iver) worked on the album virutally.

    Taylor only had one in-person collaborator for folklore, her long time boyfriend Joe Alwyn, who is credited under a pseudonym: William Bowery, and actually earned a grammy for his involvement.

    folklore explores ideas and imagery that Taylor had not previously explored in her music. The album is wistful, nostalgic, and mournful, a major departure from her previous 3.5 80s synth pop albums. It isn't too far, however, from her country music roots. In the Disney+ Long Pond sessions, Taylor talks about how she was inspired by old country music, specifically in "The Last Great American Dynasty" which uses an old country narrative device of inserting the narrator in the story via a twist at the end.

    "Betty" is another track that has heavy country music influence, featuring a harmonica and Taylor's old fake twangy accent. "Betty" is also part of a musical love triangle between "Betty", "Cardigan", and "August", which describes the same event from three different perspectives at three different times in their lives.

    Coming as a shock to absolutely no one, folklore won a Grammy for Album of the Year at the 2021 Grammy awards, following an incredible run of broken records. It broke the record for most opening-day streams for a female artist, a record which would again be broken by Rad (Taylor's Version). folklore became Taylor's seventh consecutive #1 album, making her the fourth woman to ever score seven #1 albums, joining the likes of Barbra Streisand, Madonna, and Janet Jackson.

    Taylor's surprise drop of folklore cemented her spot as one of the songwriting greats, and answers the frequently asked but incredibly shallow question, "what will you do when you can't write break up songs anymore?"
